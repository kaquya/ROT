# ROT – Architecture & Environment Design

Version: 1.0  
Status: Franchise Environment Design Standard

---

# 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the architectural and environmental design principles used throughout the ROT franchise.

Outbreak zones in ROT must feel like **real locations that existed before the Rot outbreak**.

The goal is to ensure environments feel authentic and lived-in rather than artificially constructed game levels.

This document establishes:

• architectural realism guidelines  
• environment composition rules  
• district identity principles  
• environmental storytelling integration  
• navigation and exploration design  

All environments across the franchise should follow these guidelines.

---

# 2. Environment Design Philosophy

ROT environments are built around the idea that the player is exploring **a real place that has been disrupted by biological disaster**.

The environment must communicate three stages of history:

1. The location before the outbreak  
2. The moment of collapse  
3. The Rot ecosystem takeover  

Players should be able to reconstruct what happened by observing the environment.

---

# 3. Real-World Inspiration

ROT environments should draw inspiration from real-world locations.

Examples include:

• coastal towns  
• fishing ports  
• research facilities  
• hospitals  
• forest ranger stations  
• industrial docks  

Using real-world references helps ensure environments feel believable.

Architectural scale, building placement, and infrastructure should follow real-world logic.

---

# 4. Environmental Authenticity

Every environment should appear functional before the outbreak occurred.

Buildings must reflect their intended purpose.

Examples include:

Residential Buildings  
Contain bedrooms, kitchens, personal belongings, and family objects.

Industrial Locations  
Contain machinery, storage areas, maintenance equipment.

Medical Facilities  
Contain examination rooms, patient areas, medical equipment.

Research Facilities  
Contain laboratories, data stations, containment areas.

Environments should never feel empty or artificially staged.

---

# 5. District Identity

Each major location within a ROT game should have a distinct identity.

District identity helps players orient themselves within the world.

Examples include:

Residential District  
Small homes, quiet streets, community spaces.

Downtown District  
Shops, restaurants, commercial buildings.

Industrial Harbor  
Warehouses, cranes, dock equipment.

Medical Facilities  
Hospitals, clinics, emergency centers.

Research Complexes  
Laboratories, secure facilities, underground areas.

Clear district identity helps environments remain memorable.

---

# 6. Layout Principles

ROT environments should avoid rigid or symmetrical layouts.

Real locations develop organically rather than through perfect planning.

Design features should include:

• irregular street patterns  
• varied building sizes  
• side paths and service areas  
• layered architecture

This creates more natural exploration spaces.

---

# 7. Environmental Navigation

Navigation through environments should feel intuitive.

Players should be able to understand where they are through environmental cues.

Examples include:

• landmarks  
• unique buildings  
• distinct environmental features

Navigation should rely on observation rather than excessive map markers.

---

# 8. Landmark Design

Landmarks are visually distinctive structures that help players orient themselves.

Examples include:

• lighthouse towers  
• clock towers  
• radio towers  
• water tanks  
• large industrial cranes

Landmarks should be visible from multiple locations within a district.

This allows players to navigate without constantly checking a map.

---

# 9. Exploration Loops

Each district should contain multiple exploration loops.

An exploration loop is a path that eventually reconnects to a previously visited location.

Exploration loops create satisfying navigation experiences by allowing players to:

• discover shortcuts  
• unlock previously inaccessible routes  
• revisit earlier areas more efficiently

This structure prevents environments from feeling linear.

---

# 10. Shortcut Systems

Shortcuts are an essential element of survival horror design.

Examples include:

• locked gates that can later be opened  
• ladders that create vertical connections  
• doors unlocked from the opposite side  
• collapsed walls creating new passages

Shortcuts allow players to move quickly between areas once discovered.

This reduces frustration during backtracking.

---

# 11. Environmental Density

Environment density should vary to create pacing.

Different areas should evoke different emotional responses.

Examples include:

Open Environments  
Provide temporary relief from tension.

Narrow Corridors  
Create claustrophobic tension.

Complex Interior Spaces  
Encourage exploration and navigation.

This variation prevents environments from feeling repetitive.

---

# 12. Verticality

Environments should occasionally include vertical movement.

Examples include:

• stairwells  
• rooftops  
• balconies  
• underground tunnels

Vertical exploration creates more complex spatial navigation.

However, verticality should remain manageable for solo development.
