# 1. Overview

This document defines the fundamental level design principles used throughout ROT.

These principles ensure that all environments support the game's core gameplay goals, including:

- exploration
- survival tension
- environmental storytelling
- ecological horror

Level design should reinforce the feeling that Blackwater Cove is a **real coastal town gradually consumed by the Rot ecosystem**.

These guidelines apply to all districts, interiors, outdoor environments, and setpiece locations.

---

# 2. Core Design Goals

Level design in ROT should accomplish several key goals.

### Believable Environments

All locations must feel like real places with logical architecture and environmental structure.

Buildings should reflect their intended purpose.

Examples include:

- residential homes containing personal belongings
- harbor warehouses containing fishing equipment
- hospital rooms containing medical infrastructure

The world should feel like a lived-in environment rather than a series of artificial gameplay spaces.

---

### Exploration-Driven Design

Players should be encouraged to explore environments carefully.

Exploration should reveal:

- resources
- collectibles
- narrative fragments
- environmental shortcuts

The player should rarely move through a strictly linear path.

Most environments should contain:

- branching routes
- optional rooms
- hidden side areas

---

### Survival Tension

Level layouts should reinforce the survival horror atmosphere.

This can be achieved through:

- narrow traversal spaces
- limited visibility
- unexpected enemy encounters
- environmental hazards

Players should feel vulnerable when exploring unfamiliar areas.

---

# 3. Exploration Loop Structure

ROT environments are designed around exploration loops rather than linear corridors.

A typical exploration structure includes:

```
Primary Path
     ↓
Side Area
     ↓
Resource Discovery
     ↓
Shortcut Unlock
     ↓
Return to Main Path
```

This loop encourages players to explore optional areas while maintaining forward progression.

---

# 4. Navigation and Landmarks

Players should be able to navigate environments using **visual landmarks** rather than relying on artificial navigation systems.

Examples of landmarks include:

- lighthouse structures
- clock towers
- hospital signage
- industrial cranes
- large Rot growth formations

These landmarks help players orient themselves within complex environments.

---

# 5. Environmental Storytelling

The world of ROT communicates narrative through environmental details.

Examples include:

- abandoned evacuation attempts
- collapsed barricades
- scattered research equipment
- destroyed harbor vessels

Environmental storytelling should allow players to reconstruct events without relying on direct exposition.

Narrative elements should feel naturally integrated into the environment.

---

# 6. Verticality

Vertical level design adds exploration depth and combat variety.

Examples of vertical traversal include:

- rooftops
- staircases
- balconies
- ship decks
- elevated walkways

Vertical spaces can also create opportunities for:

- stealth gameplay
- ambush encounters
- environmental puzzles

However, verticality should remain believable within the architectural context of the environment.

---

# 7. Encounter Spaces

Enemy encounters should occur within environments that support dynamic gameplay.

Encounter areas should provide:

- cover objects
- escape routes
- alternate paths

Avoid designing arenas that feel artificially constructed.

Instead, encounters should feel like they occur naturally within the environment.

---

# 8. Puzzle Integration

Puzzles should be integrated naturally into the environment.

Examples include:

- restoring power to unlock doors
- activating control panels
- retrieving key items

Puzzle elements should be clearly connected to the location where they appear.

For example:

- electrical puzzles in power stations
- medical puzzles in hospitals
- mechanical puzzles in industrial facilities

---

# 9. Safe Zone Placement

Safe zones provide temporary relief from danger.

Safe zones should be placed in locations that feel believable.

Examples include:

- residential homes
- storage rooms
- ranger cabins
- security offices

Safe zones should feel calm and secure compared to surrounding environments.

Players use safe zones to:

- save progress
- manage inventory
- review collectibles

---

# 10. Rot Environmental Integration

The Rot ecosystem should visibly alter the environment.

Examples include:

- organic growth structures covering walls
- mutated vegetation spreading through streets
- biological tunnels forming beneath buildings

Rot growth should appear **integrated with the environment**, rather than randomly placed.

The spread of Rot should gradually increase as the player progresses through the game.

---

# 11. Visibility and Lighting

Lighting plays a major role in survival horror level design.

Levels should use lighting to guide the player.

Examples include:

- illuminated doorways indicating paths
- flickering lights creating tension
- shadowed areas hiding threats

Lighting should enhance atmosphere while still allowing players to navigate environments effectively.

---

# 12. Resource Placement

Resources should be placed carefully to maintain survival tension.

Important considerations include:

- limited ammunition
- scarce healing items
- crafting materials

Resource placement should reward exploration while maintaining challenge.

Players should never feel completely safe or fully equipped.

---

# 13. District Identity

Each district of Blackwater Cove should have a distinct identity.

Examples include:

Residential District

- suburban streets
- family homes
- neighborhood parks

Harbor District

- docks
- cargo warehouses
- fishing vessels

Hospital District

- medical wards
- operating rooms
- quarantine areas

Unique district identities help players understand where they are within the world.

---

# 14. Escalation of Corruption

As the player progresses through the game, environments should show increasing Rot corruption.

Early areas

- minimal Rot presence
- subtle biological growth

Mid-game areas

- widespread contamination
- mutated wildlife

Late-game areas

- environments heavily transformed by the Rot ecosystem

This escalation reinforces the narrative progression of the outbreak.

---

# 15. Level Readability

Players should be able to understand environments quickly.

Level readability can be improved through:

- clear pathways
- distinct landmarks
- environmental lighting cues
- recognizable architectural features

Good readability reduces player confusion without removing exploration.

---

# 16. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the design principles guiding all environments in ROT.

These principles ensure that level design remains consistent across the entire game world.

By following these guidelines, designers can create environments that support:

- exploration
- survival gameplay
- narrative discovery
- ecological horror themes

These principles help ensure that Blackwater Cove feels like a coherent and believable location gradually transformed by the Rot ecosystem.
