# 1. Overview

The Audio Gameplay System defines how sound is used to support gameplay, tension, and environmental immersion.

Audio in ROT is not only atmospheric. It is also an important gameplay tool. Players rely on sound to detect nearby threats, understand environmental conditions, and anticipate danger.

The audio system contributes directly to the survival horror experience by shaping tension and guiding player awareness.

Gameplay audio can be divided into three major categories:

- enemy audio cues  
- environmental audio  
- tension sound design  

These elements work together to create a soundscape that communicates both information and atmosphere.

---

# 2. Audio Design Philosophy

The audio design of ROT follows several core principles.

## Environmental Presence

The world should feel alive through sound. Even when no enemies are present, the environment should contain subtle ambient audio that communicates the state of the ecosystem.

## Information Through Sound

Sound should provide useful information to the player.

Players should be able to interpret audio cues to understand nearby threats or environmental changes.

## Gradual Tension

Audio should gradually build tension rather than constantly overwhelming the player.

Moments of silence can be just as powerful as intense sound.

## Ecological Identity

The Rot ecosystem should have a distinctive auditory identity. Biological environments should produce sounds that feel organic, unfamiliar, and unsettling.

---

# 3. Enemy Audio Cues

Enemy audio cues allow players to detect and interpret creature behavior through sound.

Many encounters in ROT begin with audio cues before the enemy becomes visible.

Common enemy audio signals include:

- distant creature vocalizations  
- movement sounds within the environment  
- breathing or biological activity  
- attack preparation sounds  

These cues help players identify nearby threats and adjust their behavior accordingly.

---

## 3.1 Detection Sounds

Enemies may produce sounds when they detect unusual activity.

Examples include:

- alert vocalizations  
- sudden movement sounds  
- aggressive biological responses  

These sounds communicate that an enemy has noticed the player or is becoming suspicious.

---

## 3.2 Combat Sounds

During combat encounters, enemies produce audio feedback that reflects their actions.

Examples include:

- attack sounds  
- injury reactions  
- aggressive vocalizations  
- environmental destruction during movement  

Combat audio helps players track enemy behavior during tense situations.

---

# 4. Environmental Audio

Environmental audio defines the background soundscape of the world.

These sounds communicate the condition of the environment and reinforce immersion.

Examples of environmental audio include:

- wind and weather sounds  
- distant ocean waves near coastal areas  
- creaking structures within abandoned buildings  
- dripping water or unstable infrastructure  
- subtle biological activity in Rot affected areas  

Environmental audio should vary depending on location and level of contamination.

---

## 4.1 Rot Ecosystem Sounds

Areas heavily affected by the Rot should produce distinctive biological sounds.

Examples include:

- organic growth movement  
- biological pulses or rhythmic activity  
- faint insect or parasite movement  
- distant creature activity within the ecosystem  

These sounds reinforce the sense that the environment itself has become a living system.

---

# 5. Tension Sound Design

Tension sound design controls how audio builds psychological pressure on the player.

Sound can influence how players perceive danger even before threats appear.

Examples of tension design techniques include:

- subtle low frequency tones during dangerous moments  
- sudden silence before encounters  
- distant movement sounds that suggest unseen threats  
- environmental audio that gradually becomes more unsettling  

These techniques help maintain a sense of unease throughout exploration.

---

# 6. Directional Audio

Directional audio allows players to identify where sounds originate.

Accurate spatial sound design is important for gameplay.

Players should be able to determine:

- the direction of enemy movement  
- the location of environmental hazards  
- the proximity of Rot organisms  

Directional sound encourages careful listening and environmental awareness.

---

# 7. Dynamic Audio Response

Audio should adapt to gameplay situations.

Examples include:

- increasing intensity during combat encounters  
- quieter ambient audio during safe exploration  
- tension cues when enemies are nearby but unseen  

Dynamic audio helps maintain immersion and supports gameplay pacing.

---

# 8. Silence as a Tool

Silence is an important element of horror sound design.

Moments of quiet exploration allow players to focus on subtle environmental sounds.

Silence also increases the impact of sudden audio events such as creature attacks or environmental disturbances.

Careful use of silence helps prevent audio fatigue and strengthens tension.

---

# 9. Integration with Other Systems

The Audio Gameplay System interacts with several gameplay systems.

Examples include:

- the Stealth System where noise influences enemy detection  
- the Enemy AI System which reacts to sound cues  
- the Environment Design System which defines location based soundscapes  
- the Rot Exposure System where biological contamination may alter perception

These connections ensure that audio supports both gameplay and atmosphere.

---

# 10. Purpose of This Document

This document defines how audio supports gameplay in ROT.

Enemy cues, environmental sounds, and tension design work together to create a soundscape that enhances immersion and informs player decision making.

Following these guidelines ensures that audio remains a key part of both the survival mechanics and the horror atmosphere of the game.
