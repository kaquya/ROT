# ROT – Gameplay Systems (Franchise Level)

Version: 1.0  
Status: Core Systems Definition

---

# 1. System Philosophy

Gameplay systems in ROT are designed to reinforce the central themes of survival horror, environmental discovery, and resource tension.

Unlike action-heavy shooters, ROT emphasizes **careful decision-making, environmental awareness, and survival planning**.

Players are capable of fighting enemies, but combat is not always the optimal strategy.

Every gameplay system exists to support three core pillars:

• Survival tension  
• Environmental exploration  
• Tactical decision making

These systems are designed to remain consistent across all ROT games, though individual titles may expand or modify specific mechanics depending on the protagonist and setting.

---

# 2. Combat System

Combat in ROT is designed to be **dangerous, deliberate, and resource-driven**.

The player is capable of defending themselves but is rarely strong enough to defeat every enemy encountered.

Combat should feel like a survival tool rather than a power fantasy.

Encounters force players to decide whether to:

• fight enemies directly  
• avoid encounters  
• conserve resources for later threats  

---

## 2.1 Combat Design Principles

### Scarcity

Weapons and ammunition are intentionally limited.

Players should rarely feel fully stocked with supplies.

Every bullet fired represents a meaningful choice.

---

### Vulnerability

Player characters are not heavily armored soldiers.

Enemies can overwhelm the player if approached recklessly.

Poor positioning or panic can quickly lead to death.

---

### Tactical Decision Making

Each encounter should offer multiple options:

• direct combat  
• stealth avoidance  
• retreat and repositioning  
• environmental interaction  

Encounters should feel dynamic rather than scripted.

---

# 3. Weapon Systems

Weapons in ROT are designed to be **simple but impactful**.

The player typically carries a small number of weapons at any given time.

Weapons fall into several categories.

---

## 3.1 Firearms

Firearms serve as the primary ranged combat tools.

Examples include:

• handgun  
• shotgun  
• hunting rifle  
• improvised firearms  

Firearms offer reliable damage but consume valuable ammunition.

Because ammunition is scarce, players must choose carefully when to engage enemies.

---

## 3.2 Melee Weapons

Melee weapons provide close-range defense when ammunition is limited.

Examples include:

• knives  
• pipes  
• axes  
• improvised tools  

Melee combat is inherently riskier than ranged combat.

Enemies may retaliate quickly at close range.

Melee weapons therefore serve as emergency tools rather than primary weapons.

---

## 3.3 Environmental Weapons

Certain environmental elements can be used to damage enemies.

Examples include:

• explosive containers  
• electrical hazards  
• collapsing structures  
• environmental traps  

These interactions reward players who observe their surroundings carefully.

Environmental combat allows players to defeat enemies while conserving ammunition.

---

# 4. Damage and Weak Points

Many enemies possess biological weak points.

Examples include:

• exposed Rot tissue  
• mutated limbs  
• vulnerable joints  
• head structures

Targeting these areas increases damage efficiency.

This encourages precise aiming rather than excessive ammunition use.

Weak points also provide visual feedback during combat.

---

# 5. Player Health System

Player survivability is intentionally limited.

The health system is designed to reinforce survival tension.

---

## 5.1 Health Pool

The player has a finite health pool representing physical condition.

Damage may come from:

• enemy attacks  
• environmental hazards  
• boss encounters  
• environmental contamination

Taking repeated damage quickly places the player in a vulnerable state.

---

## 5.2 Healing Items

Healing items restore lost health.

Examples include:

• bandages  
• medical injections  
• emergency medical kits  

Healing supplies are intentionally rare.

Players must decide whether to heal immediately or conserve supplies.

---

# 6. Inventory System

The inventory system forces players to make strategic decisions about which items to carry.

Inventory space is intentionally limited.

Players must balance:

• weapons  
• ammunition  
• healing items  
• crafting materials  
• key items  

Every item occupies space, meaning every item carried represents a meaningful choice.

---

## 6.1 Inventory Capacity

Inventory capacity is limited by a fixed number of item slots.

Example baseline capacity:

12 slots

Items may stack depending on type, but space remains limited.

This system encourages:

• preparation before exploration  
• prioritization of valuable items  
• difficult decisions during long exploration segments

---

## 6.2 Item Categories

Items fall into several categories.

---

### Weapons

Weapons occupy inventory slots and may require ammunition.

Carrying multiple weapons reduces available space for other supplies.

---

### Ammunition

Ammunition is required for firearms.

Examples include:

• handgun rounds  
• shotgun shells  
• rifle cartridges  

Ammunition typically stacks but still occupies inventory space.

---

### Healing Items

Healing items restore health.

Examples include:

• bandages  
• medical kits  
• chemical stabilizers  

Because healing supplies are rare, they represent highly valuable inventory items.

---

### Crafting Materials

Crafting materials allow players to create useful items.

Examples include:

• chemical compounds  
• medical supplies  
• mechanical components  

These materials occupy inventory slots until used.

---

### Key Items

Key items are required for progression.

Examples include:

• access cards  
• keys  
• puzzle objects  
• mechanical components  

Key items remain in the inventory until their purpose has been fulfilled.

---

# 7. Crafting System

Crafting allows players to create useful items using materials discovered during exploration.

Crafting supports survival gameplay by providing limited flexibility without eliminating resource scarcity.

Materials remain limited and occupy inventory space, meaning crafting decisions must be made carefully.

Crafting abilities may vary between games depending on the protagonist’s background and skills.

---

## 7.1 Character-Based Crafting

Crafting capabilities are influenced by the protagonist’s profession and experience.

Examples include:

Medical Professionals  
Can craft advanced healing items or biological stabilizers.

Engineers or Mechanics  
Can craft weapon modifications or mechanical tools.

Survivalists  
May craft traps, field equipment, or improvised gear.

This system allows each ROT game to introduce new crafting options that reflect the protagonist’s identity.

---

## 7.2 Recipe Discovery

Crafting recipes are not automatically available.

Players discover recipes through exploration.

Common sources include:

• research documents  
• survivor notes  
• environmental observation  
• experimentation

Once discovered, recipes remain permanently available to the player.

---

## 7.3 Crafting Outcomes

Common crafting results include:

• healing items  
• ammunition  
• weapon upgrades  
• environmental tools  
• biological stabilizers

Crafting supplements resource scarcity rather than replacing exploration.

Players cannot rely entirely on crafting to maintain supplies.

---

# 8. Stealth System

Stealth allows players to avoid unnecessary combat.

Many enemies are dangerous when confronted directly.

Stealth mechanics give players alternative survival strategies.

These mechanics emphasize observation and patience.

---

## 8.1 Visibility

Enemies detect the player based on visual exposure.

Factors influencing visibility include:

• lighting conditions  
• distance from enemies  
• player movement speed  
• environmental cover

Dark environments provide better concealment.

Moving slowly reduces the chance of detection.

---

## 8.2 Sound

Noise plays an important role in enemy detection.

Actions that generate sound include:

• running  
• firing weapons  
• breaking objects  
• interacting with loud machinery

Enemies may investigate unusual noises.

Players can reduce noise by crouching or moving carefully.

---

## 8.3 Distraction Mechanics

Players may create distractions to manipulate enemy behavior.

Examples include:

• throwing objects  
• activating machinery  
• triggering environmental sounds

Distractions create opportunities to:

• bypass enemies  
• escape dangerous areas  
• reposition during encounters

---

# 9. Enemy AI

Enemy behavior systems are designed to create dynamic and unpredictable encounters.

Enemies respond to environmental stimuli such as movement and sound.

Rather than relying on scripted encounters, enemy behavior reacts to player actions.

---

## 9.1 Detection Systems

Enemies detect the player using two primary senses.

Sight

Enemies detect movement within their field of vision.

Sound

Enemies react to noise generated by player movement or combat.

---

## 9.2 Awareness States

Enemy behavior changes depending on their awareness of the player.

Typical states include:

Idle  
The enemy remains passive or wanders within a small area.

Suspicious  
The enemy investigates unusual sounds or disturbances.

Searching  
The enemy actively looks for the player.

Aggressive  
The enemy directly attacks the player.

These states create encounters that evolve depending on player behavior.

---

## 9.3 Patrol Behavior

Some enemies patrol environments rather than remaining stationary.

Patrol routes create moving threats that players must observe carefully.

Players may choose to:

• avoid patrol routes  
• wait for safe openings  
• engage enemies directly

---

## 9.4 Group Behavior

Certain enemy types attack in groups.

Group behavior may include:

• surrounding the player  
• converging on loud combat locations  
• attacking from multiple angles

This prevents players from easily isolating enemies.

---

# 10. Resource Economy

Resource distribution is a critical component of survival horror design.

ROT uses controlled scarcity to maintain tension throughout gameplay.

Players should rarely feel completely safe or fully equipped.

---

## 10.1 Ammunition Scarcity

Ammunition is intentionally limited.

Players must carefully decide when to engage enemies.

Fighting every enemy encountered is rarely sustainable.

Conserving ammunition is often necessary for later encounters.

---

## 10.2 Healing Item Scarcity

Healing supplies are rare and valuable.

Players must decide when healing is necessary.

Using healing items too frequently may leave the player vulnerable later.

---

## 10.3 Resource Distribution

Resources are placed throughout the environment.

Common discovery locations include:

• abandoned buildings  
• hidden containers  
• puzzle rewards  
• defeated enemies

Exploration is the primary method for obtaining supplies.

---

# 11. Difficulty Systems

ROT includes adjustable difficulty modes that affect gameplay tension.

Difficulty settings modify several factors while maintaining the same core mechanics.

---

## 11.1 Resource Availability

Higher difficulty settings reduce the amount of available supplies.

Lower difficulty settings may increase resource availability slightly.

---

## 11.2 Enemy Aggression

Enemy behavior may change depending on difficulty.

Higher difficulties may include:

• faster enemy reactions  
• increased detection sensitivity  
• more aggressive pursuit behavior

Enemy mechanics remain consistent across difficulties.

---

## 11.3 Player Margin for Error

Higher difficulty levels reduce the player's margin for error.

Examples include:

• increased enemy damage  
• slower healing effectiveness  
• fewer available supplies

Lower difficulty settings provide more forgiveness.

---

# 12. Core Gameplay Loop

The ROT gameplay loop combines exploration, survival decision-making, and environmental discovery.

Players repeatedly engage in a cycle of activities that gradually reveal the story and increase tension.

---

## 12.1 Exploration

Players explore interconnected environments searching for:

• resources  
• key items  
• narrative clues  
• new pathways

Exploration rewards curiosity and observation.

---

## 12.2 Encounter Management

Players encounter hostile organisms throughout exploration.

Players must decide whether to:

• fight enemies  
• avoid encounters  
• retreat and regroup

---

## 12.3 Resource Management

Collected resources must be carefully managed.

Players must decide:

• what items to carry  
• when to use healing supplies  
• when to conserve ammunition

---

## 12.4 Safe Zone Preparation

Safe zones provide temporary safety.

Players can:

• save progress  
• store items  
• craft supplies  
• prepare for upcoming encounters

---

## 12.5 Progression

Progression occurs through:

• unlocking new areas  
• solving environmental puzzles  
• defeating key enemies

As the player progresses, enemy encounters become more dangerous and environments become more corrupted by the Rot ecosystem.
