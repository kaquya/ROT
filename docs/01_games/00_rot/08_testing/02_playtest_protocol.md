# Playtest Protocol

# 1. Overview

Playtesting is the process of observing players interacting with the game in order to evaluate gameplay experience.

Playtesting helps identify issues that may not be visible during development testing.

Examples include:

- confusing level layouts
- unclear puzzles
- difficulty spikes
- combat balance problems
- pacing issues
- player frustration points

Because ROT is a solo-developed project, playtesting must be structured to produce useful feedback with minimal overhead.

---

# 2. Playtesting Goals

The primary goals of playtesting include:

Evaluate gameplay clarity  
Test level navigation  
Assess combat balance  
Evaluate puzzle difficulty  
Measure tension and atmosphere  
Identify confusing mechanics

Playtesting focuses on the **player experience rather than technical correctness**.

---

# 3. Types of Playtests

Different types of playtests occur throughout development.

Internal Playtesting

Performed by the developer during development.

Purpose:

Verify systems function correctly during gameplay.

---

Focused System Playtests

Testing specific systems.

Examples:

Combat testing  
Enemy AI testing  
Puzzle interaction testing

---

Full Playthrough Tests

Testing the game from beginning to end.

Purpose:

Evaluate pacing and progression.

---

External Playtests

Testing performed by players unfamiliar with the game.

Purpose:

Reveal usability issues and unclear mechanics.

---

# 4. Playtest Preparation

Before a playtest begins, the build must be prepared.

Preparation includes:

Ensuring the game launches correctly  
Removing developer debug tools if necessary  
Confirming save functionality works  
Preparing a clean playthrough environment

The playtest build should represent the current development milestone.

---

# 5. Playtest Session Structure

A typical playtest session follows a simple structure.

Introduction

Brief explanation of the game and controls.

Play Session

The player plays the game with minimal developer assistance.

Observation

The developer observes player behavior.

Feedback Collection

The player provides feedback after the session.

This structure allows natural gameplay behavior to emerge.

---

# 6. Observation Guidelines

During playtesting, the developer should observe player behavior without interfering.

Important observations include:

Player hesitation  
Repeated mistakes  
Confusion about objectives  
Unexpected strategies  
Navigation difficulties

These observations reveal design problems that players may not verbally report.

---

# 7. Player Behavior Indicators

Certain behaviors often indicate design issues.

Examples include:

Player repeatedly searching the same area  
Player ignoring obvious puzzle elements  
Player wasting large amounts of ammunition  
Player missing important environmental cues

These signals indicate that gameplay systems may require adjustment.

---

# 8. Playtest Data Recording

Playtest sessions should record important data.

Data may include:

Session duration  
Number of player deaths  
Puzzle completion times  
Enemy encounter outcomes  
Player navigation patterns

Recording this information helps evaluate gameplay balance.

---

# 9. Feedback Collection

After a playtest session, the player should be asked several questions.

Example questions include:

Was anything confusing?  
Were puzzles understandable?  
Did combat feel fair?  
Were enemies too difficult?  
Did the environment feel immersive?

Player feedback provides valuable insight into gameplay experience.

---

# 10. Difficulty Evaluation

Playtests must evaluate whether the game difficulty feels appropriate.

Signs of difficulty imbalance include:

Players dying repeatedly in the same encounter  
Players running out of resources too quickly  
Players defeating enemies too easily

Difficulty adjustments may involve:

Enemy health changes  
Enemy damage adjustments  
Resource distribution changes

---

# 11. Horror Tension Evaluation

ROT is a survival horror game, so playtesting must evaluate whether the game creates tension and suspense.

Key indicators of effective horror include:

Players moving cautiously  
Players reacting to distant sounds  
Players hesitating before entering dark areas  
Players expressing fear or tension during encounters

If players feel overly comfortable, the atmosphere may need adjustment.

Possible improvements include:

reducing lighting levels  
increasing enemy unpredictability  
improving environmental audio cues  
adjusting pacing between encounters

Horror relies heavily on pacing and anticipation.

---

# 12. Pacing Analysis

Playtests must evaluate the pacing of the game.

Pacing refers to how gameplay alternates between:

exploration  
combat  
puzzle solving  
story moments

Poor pacing may result in:

too many enemy encounters in a short time  
long periods without meaningful gameplay  
sudden difficulty spikes

Good pacing gradually builds tension while allowing players brief moments of relief.

---

# 13. Puzzle Clarity Testing

Puzzle systems must be tested to ensure players understand what to do.

Indicators of puzzle problems include:

Players wandering aimlessly  
Players repeatedly interacting with unrelated objects  
Players abandoning puzzle attempts

Puzzle improvements may include:

clearer visual clues  
environmental hints  
simplified puzzle mechanics

Players should feel challenged, but not confused.

---

# 14. Encounter Balance Testing

Enemy encounters must be evaluated for fairness and challenge.

Playtesting should examine:

enemy placement  
enemy group sizes  
available player resources  
escape routes

Encounters should encourage strategic thinking without overwhelming the player.

Example encounter evaluation:

Location  
Hospital Corridor

Enemies  
2 Medical Rotters and 1 Shadow Stalker

Test goal

Ensure the player can survive using stealth or combat.

---

# 15. Navigation Testing

Level navigation must remain intuitive.

Playtests should verify that players can:

understand the layout of each district  
identify important landmarks  
locate objectives without excessive confusion

If players frequently become lost, level design may require additional visual guidance.

Examples include:

distinct lighting  
architectural landmarks  
environmental signs

Navigation clarity is essential for maintaining immersion.

---

# 16. Resource Economy Testing

Playtesting must confirm that resource distribution is balanced.

Tests should evaluate:

ammo availability  
healing item frequency  
crafting material availability

Players should occasionally feel low on resources but not completely helpless.

Resource scarcity creates tension, but extreme scarcity may frustrate players.

---

# 17. Playtest Reporting

After each playtest session, a report should be created.

The report may include:

playtest date  
game build version  
session duration  
major issues discovered  
player feedback summary

Example report format:

Playtest Date: 2026-03-10
Build Version: v0.4
Session Duration: 45 minutes

Major Issues:
Player confused about fuse puzzle
Enemy pathfinding glitch in warehouse

Player Feedback:
Combat felt tense
Hospital area very atmospheric

Reports help track design improvements over time.

---

# 18. Iteration Process

Playtesting should directly influence development decisions.

Typical iteration process:

Playtest  
Identify problems  
Adjust systems or level design  
Test again

Frequent iteration improves gameplay quality.

---

# 19. External Playtesting Preparation

When the game reaches a stable state, external playtests should occur.

External testers should ideally be players unfamiliar with the game.

External testing reveals issues that the developer may overlook due to familiarity with the design.

External playtests may occur during later development stages.

---

# 20. Playtesting Philosophy

Playtesting must remain a continuous process.

Key principles include:

Observe player behavior carefully  
Listen to player feedback  
Identify recurring issues  
Improve the game through iteration

Consistent playtesting ensures that ROT delivers a polished and engaging player experience.
