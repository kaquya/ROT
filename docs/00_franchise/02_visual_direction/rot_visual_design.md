## 1. Overview

This document defines the visual design rules for the Rot and all Rot related biological corruption within the ROT universe.

The purpose of this document is to ensure that Rot growth, mutation, and environmental corruption follow consistent visual logic across all environments, creatures, and biological structures.

The Rot should appear as a biological system that restructures organisms and environments rather than simply destroying them. Visual elements should communicate growth, adaptation, and ecological integration.

Artists should treat Rot corruption as a living biological system that spreads through environments and restructures existing materials.

---

## 2. Core Visual Principles

Rot visual design is guided by several fundamental principles.

### Biological Integration

Rot growth should appear integrated with existing biological or structural material.

Growth often merges with bones, flesh, vegetation, or structural surfaces such as concrete, metal, and wood.

The Rot rarely appears as an isolated organism. Instead, it grows through or around existing structures.

### Organic Functionality

Mutations should appear functional rather than random.

Even distorted or unfamiliar structures should look as if they serve a biological purpose within the ecosystem.

Examples include reinforced limbs, sensory growths, or structures that appear to support reproduction or environmental expansion.

### Environmental Adaptation

Rot growth often reflects the surrounding environment.

Mutations that appear in forest environments may resemble fungal or plant structures. Mutations that appear in urban areas may integrate with architecture or infrastructure.

This helps Rot ecosystems appear responsive to their environment.

### Slow Biological Expansion

Rot growth should visually suggest gradual expansion rather than sudden explosive change.

Layers of growth, organic textures, and partially integrated structures help communicate that corruption develops over time.

---

## 3. Mutation Shapes

Rot mutation produces several recognizable shape patterns.

Artists should maintain these patterns to preserve visual consistency.

### Branching Growth

Branching structures resemble roots, veins, or fungal networks.

These structures often spread across surfaces or extend outward from host organisms.

Branching growth commonly appears in environmental Rot formations.

### Bulbous Structures

Rot tissue frequently forms swollen or rounded growths.

These structures may represent biological storage, reproduction, or nutrient processing.

Bulbous growths are common in heavily corrupted environments.

### Tendril Extensions

Thin organic tendrils may extend from Rot structures or mutated organisms.

These tendrils can serve sensory, locomotion, or attachment functions.

Tendrils often appear in early stage Rot growth.

### Structural Reinforcement

Rot may reinforce bones, limbs, or environmental structures with hardened organic material.

These reinforcements may appear as layered plates, rib like structures, or thickened tissue.

---

## 4. Biological Patterns

Rot growth tends to follow several recognizable biological patterns.

These patterns should appear repeatedly across environments and creatures.

### Vein Networks

Organic veins or branching lines may spread across surfaces, suggesting internal biological circulation.

These veins often appear slightly raised or embedded within surrounding material.

### Layered Growth

Rot structures often appear layered, with older growth partially covered by newer biological material.

Layering helps communicate that the corruption develops over time.

### Hybrid Tissue

Rot frequently merges multiple biological textures.

Examples include:

- plant like tissue merging with muscle fibers  
- fungal structures embedded in bone  
- organic membranes covering structural materials  

These hybrid structures reinforce the idea that the Rot reorganizes biological systems.

---

## 5. Growth Logic

Rot growth follows several observable rules that should guide visual design.

### Host Conversion

Rot corruption often begins by converting existing organisms or structures into biological material.

This process may involve:

- organic growth spreading across surfaces  
- biological tissue replacing existing materials  
- mutation altering the shape of the host structure  

### Expansion from Core Structures

Many Rot environments contain central biological structures that appear to act as growth origins.

These structures may resemble large biological masses, root systems, or mutated organisms embedded within the environment.

Growth often spreads outward from these core structures.

### Resource Consumption

Rot growth appears to consume available organic and structural material.

Buildings, vegetation, and biological remains may gradually become integrated into expanding Rot structures.

---

## 6. Corruption Stages

Rot corruption generally progresses through several visual stages.

These stages help artists represent different levels of environmental infection.

### Stage 1: Early Contamination

Small organic growth appears on surfaces.

Examples include:

- thin tendrils
- small fungal growths
- subtle biological discoloration

At this stage, environments remain mostly recognizable.

### Stage 2: Structural Integration

Rot growth begins merging with environmental structures.

Examples include:

- organic material spreading across walls and floors  
- small mutated organisms appearing in the ecosystem  
- biological veins spreading through surfaces  

The environment begins to feel biologically altered.

### Stage 3: Ecological Transformation

Rot ecosystems begin to dominate the environment.

Examples include:

- large biological structures replacing architecture  
- heavily mutated organisms appearing frequently  
- organic terrain replacing traditional ground surfaces  

Human structures become secondary to the new ecosystem.

### Stage 4: Full Rot Ecosystem

The environment becomes a stable Rot ecosystem.

Examples include:

- large interconnected biological structures  
- complete transformation of terrain and structures  
- complex predator and prey relationships between mutated organisms  

At this stage, the environment no longer resembles its original form.

---

## 7. Interaction with Human Structures

Rot corruption often integrates with human infrastructure.

Examples include:

- organic growth spreading through buildings  
- biological material reinforcing collapsed structures  
- Rot ecosystems forming inside abandoned facilities  
- infrastructure becoming partially biological

Human architecture should remain visible within these environments, but it becomes increasingly absorbed by the biological ecosystem.

---

## 8. Visual Restraint

Rot corruption should remain grounded in biological plausibility.

Excessive exaggeration should be avoided.

Mutations should appear unsettling because they feel possible rather than because they appear fantastical.

Maintaining restraint ensures that Rot environments remain believable and atmospheric.

---

## 9. Purpose of This Document

This document provides visual rules for designing Rot corruption, mutated organisms, and Rot environments.

Following these principles ensures that all Rot related visuals across the franchise remain consistent and biologically grounded.

These guidelines should be used by environment artists, creature designers, and concept artists when creating Rot affected content.
