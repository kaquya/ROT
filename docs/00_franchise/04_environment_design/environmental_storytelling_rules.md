## 1. Overview

Environmental storytelling is one of the primary narrative tools used in the ROT universe.

Instead of relying heavily on direct exposition, the world itself communicates information about past events, human activity, scientific experiments, and the spread of the Rot.

Players should be able to understand parts of the story by observing environments, objects, and biological changes in the world.

The purpose of this document is to define the rules that guide how narrative elements are placed within environments.

---

## 2. Core Principles

Environmental storytelling in ROT follows several key principles.

### Natural Discovery

Story elements should feel naturally integrated into the environment rather than deliberately staged for the player.

Scenes should appear as if they were left behind during real events.

### Visual Clarity

Important narrative details should be visually readable.

Players should be able to understand what happened in a location through objects, positioning, and environmental context.

### Subtlety

Environmental storytelling should avoid over explaining events.

Scenes should provide clues rather than complete explanations.

### Consistency

All environmental storytelling should follow the biological and ecological logic of the Rot ecosystem.

Events depicted in the environment should align with the world history and Rot ecology.

---

## 3. Narrative Scene Types

Several common scene types are used to communicate story events through the environment.

These scene types can appear throughout the world and should be varied depending on the location.

---

### 3.1 Corpse Scenes

Corpse scenes represent the aftermath of Rot encounters, evacuations, or failed survival attempts.

These scenes can communicate how individuals or groups encountered danger.

Examples include:

- bodies positioned near defensive positions  
- remains near collapsed escape routes  
- skeletal remains partially integrated into Rot growth  

Corpse scenes should avoid excessive gore and instead focus on the implications of what occurred.

---

### 3.2 Laboratory Remains

Research facilities and laboratories often contain evidence of scientific investigation into the Rot.

These environments can reveal important information about past experiments or containment attempts.

Examples include:

- broken laboratory equipment  
- containment chambers damaged from within  
- research documents left behind during evacuation  
- biological samples integrated into Rot growth  

Laboratory remains often indicate the presence of the Halcyon Initiative or other research groups.

---

### 3.3 Abandoned Homes

Residential environments communicate the human side of the Rot outbreak.

These locations reveal how ordinary people attempted to survive or escape the affected area.

Examples include:

- belongings left behind during evacuation  
- barricaded doors or windows  
- food supplies or survival preparations  
- personal items scattered throughout living spaces  

These scenes help convey the emotional impact of the Rot on civilian populations.

---

### 3.4 Contamination Zones

Contamination zones show areas where the Rot has begun transforming the environment.

These locations often represent early or intermediate stages of ecological restructuring.

Examples include:

- biological growth spreading across buildings  
- organic veins spreading through terrain  
- mutated vegetation emerging from urban environments  
- environmental structures partially converted into Rot tissue  

These zones visually demonstrate how the Rot spreads and alters ecosystems.

---

## 4. Layered Storytelling

Strong environmental storytelling often combines multiple narrative elements within the same location.

For example, a contaminated research lab might contain:

- damaged scientific equipment  
- notes describing failed containment attempts  
- Rot growth spreading through laboratory walls  
- skeletal remains of researchers  

These layered details allow players to reconstruct complex events by exploring the environment.

---

## 5. Environmental Progression

As players move through environments, storytelling elements should reflect the progression of Rot activity.

Earlier areas may show subtle signs of contamination.

Later areas may display complete ecological transformation.

This progression helps communicate how the Rot spreads through environments over time.

---

## 6. Integration with Gameplay

Environmental storytelling should support gameplay exploration.

Narrative elements may guide players toward important locations or encourage investigation.

Examples include:

- unusual scenes drawing player attention  
- environmental damage suggesting hidden paths  
- abandoned equipment hinting at nearby facilities  

Storytelling elements should reward careful observation.

---

## 7. Avoiding Overuse

Environmental storytelling scenes should be used carefully.

Too many staged scenes can reduce their impact.

Scenes should appear meaningful and unique rather than repetitive.

Variety helps maintain player curiosity and engagement.

---

## 8. Purpose of This Document

This document defines how narrative elements are communicated through environments within the ROT universe.

Following these guidelines ensures that environments consistently deliver story information while preserving the sense of mystery and discovery that defines ROT.
