# ROT – Art Direction Bible

Version: 1.0  
Status: Franchise Visual Identity

---

# 1. Purpose of This Document

The Art Direction Bible defines the visual identity of the ROT franchise.

Its purpose is to ensure that every environment, creature, object, and visual element follows a consistent artistic philosophy.

The document establishes:

• the visual tone of the ROT universe  
• the balance between realism and stylization  
• environmental visual rules  
• lighting philosophy  
• Rot biological design language  

This document acts as a **visual rulebook for the franchise**.

All visual elements should reinforce the central themes of ROT:

• ecological horror  
• environmental decay  
• biological reconstruction  
• isolation and unease  

---

# 2. Core Visual Pillars

The visual identity of ROT is built around five major pillars.

These pillars guide every artistic decision in the franchise.

---

## 2.1 Grounded Realism

ROT environments should feel like **real locations that existed before the outbreak**.

The world should never appear abstract or exaggerated.

Buildings, infrastructure, and objects should feel believable and functional.

Examples include:

• realistic architecture  
• believable environmental clutter  
• functional industrial equipment  
• ordinary residential interiors  

The horror of ROT emerges from the contrast between familiar environments and biological corruption.

---

## 2.2 Biological Intrusion

The Rot ecosystem visually invades environments.

This intrusion should appear **organic rather than decorative**.

Rot growths should look like they are spreading through the environment rather than being placed on top of it.

Common visual characteristics include:

• vein-like structures spreading across surfaces  
• organic tissue emerging from walls or floors  
• growths following natural structural paths  
• biological forms interacting with architecture  

Rot growth should feel like an ecosystem expanding into human spaces.

---

## 2.3 Environmental Decay

The outbreak zones in ROT are locations that have experienced sudden collapse.

Visual design should communicate that these places were abandoned during crisis events.

Environmental decay may include:

• damaged vehicles  
• broken windows  
• collapsed structures  
• abandoned belongings  
• emergency barricades  

These details reinforce the history of the location before the Rot takeover.

---

## 2.4 Environmental Transformation

As the Rot spreads, environments gradually change.

This transformation should occur in visible stages.

Early environments show small biological growth.

Mid-game areas show structural integration between Rot and architecture.

Late-game environments appear heavily dominated by biological structures.

The transformation should feel progressive rather than sudden.

---

## 2.5 Ecological Horror

The Rot ecosystem should resemble a **distorted biological system rather than a supernatural infection**.

The visual language of the Rot draws inspiration from:

• marine organisms  
• fungal growths  
• vascular tissue  
• parasitic structures  

The Rot should feel alien yet biologically plausible.

---

# 3. Realism vs Stylization

ROT aims for **grounded realism with subtle stylization**.

The world should appear believable while still supporting strong visual identity.

---

## 3.1 Realistic Environment Design

Environment design should prioritize realism.

Examples include:

• real-world building layouts  
• believable infrastructure  
• accurate environmental proportions  

This realism helps the player feel that the world once functioned normally.

---

## 3.2 Controlled Stylization

Stylization appears primarily in the Rot ecosystem.

Rot growth may display exaggerated biological structures to emphasize its alien nature.

However, stylization should never feel cartoonish or exaggerated.

The Rot should remain unsettling and organic.

---

# 4. Environmental Tone

ROT environments should evoke a sense of unease even when enemies are not present.

The world itself should feel unsettling.

Tone is created through:

• lighting  
• environmental composition  
• subtle environmental movement  
• sound design integration  

Players should feel tension simply by exploring environments.

---

# 5. Color Palette

Color plays an important role in communicating environmental transformation.

---

## 5.1 Human Environment Colors

Areas untouched by the Rot use muted natural tones.

Examples include:

• faded coastal blues  
• concrete grays  
• industrial rust tones  
• dark forest greens  

These colors reinforce the grounded realism of the world.

---

## 5.2 Rot Ecosystem Colors

As the Rot spreads, new color tones appear.

Examples include:

• deep red tissue tones  
• dark purples  
• pale fungal whites  
• sickly yellow organic fluids  

These colors contrast strongly with normal environments.

The appearance of these colors visually signals Rot contamination.

---

# 6. Lighting Philosophy

Lighting is one of the most important tools for establishing mood in ROT.

Lighting should emphasize atmosphere and tension rather than visual spectacle.

---

## 6.1 Natural Darkness

Many environments in ROT rely on low lighting.

Examples include:

• nighttime exploration  
• power outages  
• underground environments  

Darkness increases tension and forces players to rely on limited visibility.

---

## 6.2 Practical Light Sources

Lighting should often come from believable sources.

Examples include:

• street lamps  
• flashlights  
• emergency lights  
• flickering fluorescent fixtures  

These light sources reinforce realism while creating dramatic lighting conditions.

---

## 6.3 Rot Lighting

Certain Rot structures may emit faint biological illumination.

Examples include:

• glowing spores  
• pulsing growth nodes  
• bioluminescent tissue  

These light sources should appear subtle and organic.

They should never dominate the scene.

---

# 7. Environmental Composition

Environment composition guides how players visually interpret spaces.

ROT environments should encourage exploration while maintaining tension.

---

## 7.1 Visual Landmarks

Distinct landmarks help players orient themselves within environments.

Examples include:

• lighthouse structures  
• towers  
• large industrial equipment  
• distinctive buildings  

Landmarks improve navigation without requiring large map systems.

---

## 7.2 Visual Pathways

Environmental design should subtly guide players through spaces.

Examples include:

• light sources leading toward exits  
• pathways framed by environmental objects  
• architectural lines guiding player movement  

Players should feel guided without obvious direction markers.

---

## 7.3 Environmental Density

Environment density should vary to maintain pacing.

Some areas should feel open and exposed.

Others should feel claustrophobic and confined.

This variation prevents visual fatigue.

---

# 8. Rot Biological Design

The Rot ecosystem is the most visually distinctive element of the ROT franchise.

Its design must always reinforce the idea that the phenomenon is **biological rather than supernatural**.

Rot structures should resemble living tissue systems that have spread into human environments.

The Rot should feel unsettling because it appears to be **alive and growing**.

---

## 8.1 Structural Characteristics

Rot growths typically display several recognizable features.

These include:

• branching tissue structures  
• vein-like organic networks  
• layered biological membranes  
• fibrous connective tissue  
• growth nodes resembling organs

These elements create the impression that the environment has become part of a living organism.

---

## 8.2 Growth Direction

Rot growth should rarely appear random.

Instead, it should follow structural logic within the environment.

Examples include:

• spreading along walls and ceilings  
• emerging from cracks in floors  
• following building infrastructure such as pipes or cables  
• connecting multiple surfaces through tissue bridges

Growth patterns should appear purposeful rather than decorative.

---

## 8.3 Pulsation and Motion

Some Rot structures may display subtle motion.

Examples include:

• slow tissue pulsing  
• organic breathing movement  
• drifting spores  
• twitching tendrils

Movement should remain subtle to avoid visual distraction.

The goal is to create the unsettling impression that the environment itself is alive.

---

# 9. Creature Design Principles

Rot creatures are biological reconstructions created by the Rot ecosystem.

Their appearance should always reflect their biological origin.

Creatures should appear like **distorted organisms rather than traditional monsters**.

---

## 9.1 Anatomical Distortion

Rot creatures should retain visible traces of their original anatomy.

For example:

• human-based creatures retain distorted skeletal structures  
• animal-based creatures display mutated forms of recognizable species

These visual clues reinforce the biological nature of the transformation.

---

## 9.2 Mutation Logic

Mutations should follow consistent biological rules.

Common mutation patterns include:

• elongated limbs  
• fused skeletal structures  
• exposed tissue growth  
• asymmetrical body structures

These changes should appear like failed biological reconstruction attempts.

---

## 9.3 Silhouette Clarity

Enemy silhouettes must remain recognizable even in low lighting.

Each enemy type should have a distinctive outline.

Examples:

Rotters  
Unstable humanoid silhouettes.

Stalkers  
Low, predatory shapes designed for ambush.

Spliced Organisms  
Large distorted forms with multiple limbs or fused bodies.

Clear silhouettes help players identify threats quickly.

---

# 10. Environmental Storytelling Visuals

The environment itself should communicate much of the story.

Visual storytelling is a major component of ROT’s narrative design.

Players should learn about events through observation rather than explicit dialogue.

---

## 10.1 Signs of Evacuation

Outbreak zones often display evidence of failed evacuation attempts.

Examples include:

• abandoned vehicles  
• barricaded streets  
• emergency medical stations  
• hastily packed belongings

These details suggest that the outbreak occurred rapidly.

---

## 10.2 Research Evidence

Locations connected to Halcyon research often contain scientific materials.

Examples include:

• laboratory equipment  
• research notes  
• containment chambers  
• sample storage units

These clues gradually reveal how the Rot was discovered.

---

## 10.3 Survivor Evidence

Some areas show signs of human resistance or survival attempts.

Examples include:

• barricaded rooms  
• supply caches  
• emergency radios  
• improvised defenses

These details humanize the disaster and show how residents attempted to survive.

---

# 11. Environmental Transformation Stages

As the Rot ecosystem develops, environments should visibly change.

The transformation follows several stages.

---

## Stage 1 – Contamination

Minimal visual infection.

Examples include:

• small Rot growth patches  
• unusual organic residue  
• subtle environmental damage

These areas still appear mostly intact.

---

## Stage 2 – Integration

Rot structures begin merging with architecture.

Examples include:

• tissue spreading across walls  
• growth emerging from floors  
• small biological nodes forming

Enemies begin appearing more frequently.

---

## Stage 3 – Domination

The Rot ecosystem dominates the environment.

Examples include:

• massive biological structures  
• buildings fused with organic growth  
• environmental surfaces resembling living tissue

At this stage the environment itself resembles a biological organism.

---

# 12. Asset Design Strategy for Solo Development

Because ROT is designed as a solo-developed project, asset creation must remain manageable.

Several strategies help maintain visual quality without overwhelming production workload.

---

## 12.1 Modular Environment Assets

Environment pieces should be reusable across multiple districts.

Examples include:

• wall segments  
• building facades  
• street elements  
• interior props

Modular design significantly reduces asset production time.

---

## 12.2 Texture Reuse

Rot textures should be designed to work across multiple environments.

Examples include:

• organic tissue textures  
• fungal growth materials  
• biological vein networks

Reusable materials reduce the number of required unique assets.

---

## 12.3 Enemy Variants

Enemy variety can be achieved through visual variation rather than entirely new models.

Examples include:

• texture variations  
• minor anatomical changes  
• color shifts indicating mutation stages

This approach reduces modeling workload while maintaining visual diversity.

---

# 13. Visual Consistency Rules

To maintain visual identity across the franchise, several rules should always be followed.

---

## 13.1 The Rot Is Organic

The Rot must always appear biological.

It should never resemble mechanical structures or artificial technology.

---

## 13.2 Environments Were Once Normal

Every outbreak zone must feel like a real place that existed before the Rot arrived.

This contrast is critical to the horror experience.

---

## 13.3 Transformation Is Progressive

The Rot should gradually take over environments.

Players should observe increasing levels of biological corruption as they progress.

---

## 13.4 Atmosphere Over Spectacle

Visual design should prioritize atmosphere rather than large cinematic spectacle.

Subtle environmental tension is more effective than dramatic visual effects.
