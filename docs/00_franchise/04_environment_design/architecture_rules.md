## 1. Overview

This document defines the architectural rules used when designing human environments in the ROT universe.

The goal is to ensure that locations across all ROT projects feel believable, grounded, and consistent with the setting. Architecture should reflect real world logic, regional culture, and environmental conditions before the Rot begins to reshape the environment.

Human structures are an important part of ROT's environmental storytelling. Buildings, infrastructure, and urban layouts communicate the history of a place, the people who lived there, and the consequences of abandonment after Rot events.

Architecture should therefore appear functional, lived in, and gradually degraded rather than artificially constructed for gameplay.

---

## 2. Coastal Town Layouts

Many ROT environments are set in coastal settlements. These towns developed around fishing, shipping, and small scale industry.

Their layout typically follows natural terrain and access to water.

Common layout characteristics include:

- harbors and docks near the shoreline  
- dense residential districts close to the harbor  
- industrial zones near shipping areas  
- roads following coastline curves or terrain elevation  
- small commercial centers around central streets  

Buildings should appear closely spaced in older districts, reflecting gradual expansion over time.

Town growth often occurs in layers, with older buildings near the shoreline and newer development appearing further inland.

---

## 3. Infrastructure Design

Infrastructure in ROT environments should feel functional and realistic.

Infrastructure elements may include:

- roads and small highways  
- bridges and tunnels  
- docks and shipping facilities  
- water towers and pumping stations  
- electrical substations and power lines  
- rail or cargo transport infrastructure  

These systems should show clear purpose and connection to surrounding buildings and districts.

Infrastructure often becomes a key exploration path for players, guiding movement through environments.

---

## 4. Abandoned Infrastructure

In Rot affected regions, infrastructure frequently becomes abandoned or partially damaged.

Evidence of abandonment should appear throughout the environment.

Common signs include:

- vehicles left behind during evacuation  
- damaged or collapsed roads  
- blocked bridges or tunnels  
- broken electrical systems  
- empty checkpoints or quarantine barriers  

These details help communicate the sudden disruption of human activity.

Infrastructure should appear functional in the past but gradually degraded over time.

---

## 5. Structural Decay

Human structures in ROT environments should show signs of long term environmental exposure.

Decay should appear gradual and believable rather than sudden.

Typical decay features include:

- rust on metal surfaces  
- cracked concrete and damaged pavement  
- broken windows and collapsed roofs  
- water damage and mold growth  
- peeling paint and weathered wood  

Structural damage should reflect realistic environmental factors such as storms, neglect, and natural wear.

---

## 6. Rot Integration

As the Rot spreads through an environment, it begins to merge with existing architecture.

Buildings and infrastructure may become partially biological over time.

Examples include:

- organic growth spreading through walls and floors  
- biological structures emerging from collapsed buildings  
- Rot tissue replacing structural materials  
- organic root systems spreading through foundations  

Despite these transformations, the original architecture should remain partially recognizable.

This contrast between human construction and biological growth reinforces the environmental storytelling of the ROT universe.

---

## 7. Environmental Storytelling Through Architecture

Architecture should communicate narrative information without requiring direct explanation.

Buildings and infrastructure can reveal:

- how people lived in the area  
- how evacuation occurred  
- where containment attempts were made  
- how the Rot affected specific locations  

Examples include:

- research facilities built near contaminated zones  
- temporary quarantine checkpoints  
- abandoned homes showing signs of sudden departure  
- damaged laboratories or containment chambers  

These elements allow players to reconstruct past events through observation.

---

## 8. Architectural Consistency

All environments in the ROT universe should follow consistent architectural logic.

Buildings should appear functional and suited to their environment.

Districts should develop naturally based on geography, economy, and infrastructure.

Avoid placing structures without clear purpose or connection to surrounding areas.

Maintaining realistic architecture ensures that environments remain immersive and believable.

---

## 9. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the architectural guidelines used when creating environments in the ROT universe.

It ensures that towns, facilities, and infrastructure follow believable construction patterns while supporting environmental storytelling and exploration.

These rules help maintain consistency across all ROT environments.
